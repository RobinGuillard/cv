Robin Guillard, Ph.D.

I am a Postdoctoral Fellow at Stanford University (Mignot Lab), where my research focuses on identifying correlates of sleep debt through large-scale proteomics. Our goal is to establish predictive proteomics biomarkers of cognitive impairment induced by fatigue, bridging molecular biology and sleep medicine.

Originally trained as an engineer at École Polytechnique, I progressively developed a passion for neuroscience and translational research. A pivotal research semester at UC Berkeley sparked my interest in auditory neuroscience, ultimately leading me to focus on tinnitus — a condition that remains both scientifically and clinically challenging.

I received my Ph.D. in Neuroscience from the University of Grenoble Alpes in 2024, where I characterized sleep-related fluctuations in tinnitus. I discovered that a subgroup of patients whose tinnitus intensity varies with sleep likely reflects changes in REM sleep debt, with broader implications for understanding how sleep regulation interacts with sensory, cognitive, and emotional processing.

In parallel, I remain strongly committed to advancing tinnitus research and maintain close collaborations with the Institut de l'Audition in Paris. I am uniquely positioned to integrate tinnitus research, large-scale proteomics, and advanced machine learning modeling to establish the first objective diagnostic biomarker profile for tinnitus.

Research Interests

Proteomics Biomarkers of Sleep Debt

At Stanford (Mignot Lab), I work on identifying correlates of sleep debt through large-scale proteomics, analyzing datasets comprising approximately 55,000 patients and over 3,000 quantified proteins. Our team develops state-of-the-art statistical approaches integrating proteomic and genetic data to identify robust biological signatures and predictive biomarkers of cognitive impairment induced by fatigue.

Sleep-Modulated Tinnitus

During my doctoral work, I characterized previously overlooked subgroups of patients whose tinnitus intensity varies with sleep. Using clinical and polysomnographic data, I found that these variations likely reflect changes in sleep debt, and more specifically REM sleep debt. I developed machine learning approaches for tinnitus subgroup identification using clustering and semi-supervised frameworks.

Tinnitus Proteomics

Large-scale blood proteomics has recently transformed our ability to uncover molecular signatures across a broad range of medical conditions, yet its application to tinnitus remains strikingly underexplored. My postdoctoral training equips me with the analytical and computational expertise to conduct large-scale proteomic investigations aimed at establishing the first objective diagnostic tool for tinnitus.

Computational Methods

My methodological contributions span machine learning for clinical subtyping, infradian biological rhythm analysis, sympathetic and parasympathetic correlates from ECG (HR and HRV), and advanced EEG analysis techniques including rhythmic/arrhythmic spectral analysis and sleep spindle extraction using advanced machine learning techniques.
